&gt; Given your confusion about atoms and binaries below, firstly read [1], so 
&gt; you're up to speed about the various Erlang types.
&gt; 
&gt; Now, looking back at your logs, there are instances of the atom "r\\_object" at 
&gt; the start of a tuple. This is a specific record that riak uses internally to 
&gt; store both the metadata and the binary contents of a bucket-key-value item.
&gt; 
&gt; Now, a little grepping through the riak\\_kv repository [2], turns out a 
&gt; reference to this "r\\_object" atom as part of a record [3]. Records are just 
&gt; tuples with a specific atom at the start. The record specification says that 
&gt; the first field is the bucket, and the second is the key (both as binary 
&gt; data).
&gt; 
&gt; So, going back to your logs, where you found "r\\_object", the second and third 
&gt; items in those tuples are the binaries representing your bucket and your key 
&gt; (the first is the name of the record, the atom "r\\_object"). 
&gt; 
&gt; If you copy and paste them into a new erlang shell, putting a "." afterwards, 
&gt; the shell will print them out as strings, like in [4].
&gt; 
&gt; I had a headstart because I know the riak\\_kv repository and how it works, but 
&gt; hopefully this will help.
&gt; 
&gt; Yes, your nodes will start to OOM if you don't have enough RAM to fetch these 
&gt; 500-900MB items. 
&gt; 
&gt; That's why you should resolve siblings locally \\*every\\* time you read a value 
&gt; from riak, and once you've done your application-level modifications to the 
&gt; resolved object, write it back. Depending on your riak client, it will either 
&gt; have a nice resolution system, or allow you to write one yourself.
&gt;
